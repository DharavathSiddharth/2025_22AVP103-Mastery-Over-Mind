⦁	Scenario 1: I have did my mid-terms not upto my ability, and i was really upset about it.

      Date: 2 days after mid-term exams

            Vaishnavi mam(PSAT): Helped me to get over the situation and made me realise my weakness and gave me strength to improve.
            
            There are certain moments in academic life that leave a deeper mark than grades or scores. For me, one such experience came after my midterm exams. I had prepared sincerely and expected to perform well, but when I received my results, I realized that I had not performed up to my actual ability. The marks were decent, but not a true reflection of what I was capable of. I felt deeply disappointed, not just because of the grades, but because I knew I could have done much better. That realization weighed heavily on me, and I found myself replaying the exam in my head, analyzing what went wrong.At first, I blamed myself completely. I thought that maybe I had not studied well enough, or that I had failed to manage my time properly. My self-confidence took a dip, and I remember feeling quite disheartened for a few days. I kept thinking, “Why couldn’t I express what I knew in the right way?” However, this moment of discouragement soon turned into one of reflection and learning, largely because of a conversation I had with my teacher, Vaishnavi ma’am. She noticed that I was upset about my performance and took the time to talk to me personally about it. What started as a short feedback session ended up becoming one of the most valuable discussions I have ever had about self-improvement.During our conversation, Vaishnavi ma’am helped me understand that exams are not just about knowledge; they are also about how we present that knowledge under time constraints. She gently pointed out that my strength lay in the clarity of my explanations — the way I defined concepts and connected ideas showed real understanding. However, she also helped me see my major weakness: I was writing far too much for even short-answer questions. For instance, in a three-mark question, I would write half a page, elaborating every detail as if it were a long-answer essay. This habit, though it came from enthusiasm and thorough preparation, was actually working against me. Because of it, I would spend too much time on smaller questions, leaving less time for the ones that carried more marks.When she explained this to me, I realized how true it was. I remembered moments during the exam when I had rushed through the last few questions because time was running out. I also noticed how my handwriting would become messier towards the end due to that rush. What struck me most was how Vaishnavi ma’am did not criticize me harshly. Instead, she helped me see this weakness as an opportunity for growth. She said something that stayed with me: “Your ideas are good, but your time management should be as strong as your understanding.”That statement became a turning point in my perspective. I started to see the exam not as a failure, but as feedback. I was grateful that my teacher took the time to look beyond my marks and notice my potential. Gratitude replaced disappointment as I understood that my weakness wasn’t a permanent flaw — it was just a skill I hadn’t refined yet.After that day, I made a conscious effort to work on my writing strategy. I began practicing past papers under timed conditions, forcing myself to summarize my thoughts more effectively. Initially, it was difficult because I had a habit of overexplaining everything. But with practice, I learned to capture the essence of an answer in fewer words. I also realized that concise writing doesn’t mean incomplete writing — it means expressing the same clarity in a compact way. This exercise not only improved my speed but also made me a better communicator.Another important realization from this experience was about emotional resilience. It is natural to feel upset when results do not meet expectations, but what matters more is how we respond to that disappointment. I learned that being grateful during such times helps us recover faster and see the lesson hidden inside the struggle. I could have easily stayed discouraged, but instead, I chose to focus on the support and guidance I received. I began to see gratitude not as a feeling, but as a perspective — one that shifts our focus from what went wrong to what we can learn.Reflecting deeper, I recognized that gratitude towards teachers is often underestimated. Teachers like Vaishnavi ma’am do more than just grade papers — they mentor, motivate, and shape our growth. Her calm and constructive feedback helped me regain confidence and clarity. Instead of judging my performance harshly, she showed me that improvement is always possible. That conversation taught me that failure is not the opposite of success; it is part of the learning process.In the following weeks, I began applying her advice across subjects. I started writing practice answers within time limits, focusing on key points and definitions. I even developed a small personal rule — to allocate a specific number of minutes to each section of an exam. This helped me maintain pace without sacrificing quality. I also began observing my handwriting, making sure it remained legible till the end. Gradually, I noticed an improvement in both my writing speed and my overall presentation.Beyond academics, this experience also influenced my mindset. I learned that self-awareness is the first step toward progress. Recognizing both strengths and weaknesses helps us grow more holistically. I became thankful for the qualities I possessed — clarity of thought, conceptual understanding, and effort — but also grateful for the areas that challenged me, because they pushed me to become better. It reminded me that no one is perfect, and every weakness, when handled with the right attitude, can turn into a new strength.This episode also helped me redefine what “ability” truly means. Earlier, I thought ability was just about how much I knew or how intelligent I was. Now, I understand that ability includes emotional balance, time management, clarity, and adaptability. True ability is about applying what we know effectively, not just knowing it. This understanding was humbling, and it taught me to approach future exams with a calmer, more strategic mindset.In conclusion, what began as disappointment turned into a meaningful lesson in gratitude and growth. I am grateful to Vaishnavi ma’am for identifying my strengths and weaknesses with such patience and insight. Her guidance helped me see myself more clearly and gave me the motivation to improve. I learned that gratitude is not only for success but also for failure, because failure teaches us lessons that success often cannot. Now, whenever I face challenges, I remind myself of this experience — to stay calm, to stay thankful, and to keep learning.This scenario made me realize that real progress is a combination of self-reflection, feedback, and gratitude. I no longer fear underperformance; instead, I see it as a mirror reflecting what I can do better next time. With that mindset, I feel more confident and grounded. Today, I am proud not only of my improvement in exams but also of the growth in my attitude. Gratitude has taught me to transform self-criticism into self-betterment — and that lesson, I believe, is far more valuable than any grade.
            
⦁	Scenario 2: I misunderstood a conversation with my family and i felt really low upto a point were i pondered for the reason to keep moving ahead.

      Date: 1 week before mid-term exams

            My friends(Akshaya, Samiksha and sruthika) of different department: Helped me to realize my values and the achievements i have made until now. And also encouraged me that a piece of paper do not determine my future and should not make my confident go down.
            
            There are times in life when a simple misunderstanding can deeply affect our emotions and confidence. One such incident happened to me a week before my midterm examinations. It was a phase when I was already under academic pressure, trying to balance revision and expectations. At that point, I misunderstood a conversation with my family that left me feeling disheartened and emotionally drained. Looking back now, I realize that this experience, though painful, became an important lesson in gratitude, self-worth, and emotional growth.It started one evening when my family was having a discussion about educational expenses. They mentioned how much they were spending on my studies — nearly five lakh rupees per year — while my sister, who is pursuing MBBS in a government college, pays only about one lakh annually. They also spoke about how I had appeared for both JEE and NEET but didn’t manage to secure a government NEET seat. Though they didn’t mean to compare or criticize, I took those words very personally. At that moment, it felt as though I had failed to meet their expectations.In reality, they were only talking about financial realities — but I interpreted their words differently. I thought they were disappointed in me, that they saw me as a burden rather than a source of pride. My mind started overthinking. I kept replaying the conversation, twisting every sentence into self-critical thoughts: “Maybe I am not doing enough.” “Maybe they regret investing so much in me.” “Maybe I’m just not good at studies.”That evening, something inside me broke. I felt hurt and worthless. My confidence, which I had built through years of effort and persistence, suddenly felt fragile. I couldn’t bring myself to focus on my upcoming exams. I sat with my books open, but I couldn’t study. Every time I tried, those words echoed in my mind. For the first time, I began to question the very reason to keep moving ahead. The emotional weight became so heavy that even my usual optimism couldn’t pull me out of it.For the next few days, I stayed quiet and withdrawn. My motivation to study disappeared completely. I began feeling inferior to my sister and even doubted whether I deserved my current opportunity. However, life has a way of sending the right people to lift us up when we need it the most. In my case, that support came from three of my closest friends — Akshaya, Samiksha, and Shrutika — who belong to different departments.They quickly sensed that something was wrong. My usual cheerful self had turned into someone distant and lost. One afternoon, they approached me gently and asked if everything was fine. At first, I brushed it off, pretending I was just tired. But they persisted, showing genuine concern. Eventually, I broke down and told them everything — the conversation, the misunderstanding, and how it had completely shattered my confidence. I remember tears rolling down my cheeks as I spoke, feeling both relieved and embarrassed.Their response, however, was not pity. It was pure compassion. They listened patiently, without interrupting or judging me. When I finished, they reminded me of something I had forgotten — my worth and my journey. They made me recall all that I had achieved so far: clearing both JEE and NEET exams, handling tough situations independently, and staying consistent even when things didn’t go as planned. They said that not getting a government seat didn’t define my intelligence or value, and that education isn’t just about marks or ranks — it’s about perseverance and growth.What touched me most was when they said, “A piece of paper doesn’t determine your future, and no one’s opinion should control your happiness.” Those words struck a chord in me. For the first time in days, I felt seen and understood. They also reminded me that I had always been one of the most confident people they knew, and that this temporary phase didn’t erase my strength. Hearing that gave me immense reassurance. Their belief in me slowly started rebuilding my belief in myself.That evening, after talking with them, I sat alone and reflected. I realized that my family never intended to hurt me. They were simply expressing their worries about financial pressure, not comparing me with my sister or belittling my efforts. The misunderstanding had grown only because I had let my insecurities interpret their words negatively. With that realization came a deep sense of gratitude — gratitude towards my parents for their sacrifices, and gratitude towards my friends for helping me see things clearly.In the days that followed, I began preparing for my exams again. My friends continued to encourage me, sending motivational messages, checking on me, and reminding me to take breaks. Their presence became a source of emotional strength. Whenever I felt low, I recalled their words about self-worth. Slowly, my mindset shifted from guilt to gratitude. Instead of feeling sorry for not getting a government seat, I began to feel thankful that I still had the chance to pursue my studies in a good institution — something many people never get.This experience taught me several valuable lessons. First, it reminded me how powerful our thoughts can be. A simple misunderstanding can turn into emotional chaos if we let our negative thoughts take over. Second, it showed me the importance of communication. Had I calmly talked to my family right away, I would have understood their real intention sooner. Finally, it reinforced the value of gratitude — not only for success or comfort, but especially during times of emotional confusion. Gratitude brings perspective, helping us see light even in moments of darkness.

     Date: Before 3 days

           Transitioning into a completely new academic domain is never easy, especially when it is one that feels foreign to your background. Coming from a biology stream, I found myself stepping into a course that was almost entirely based on cybersecurity and computer knowledge — a world of coding, hardware, and digital systems that seemed far removed from the concepts of cells, organisms, and life sciences that I had studied before. Initially, I felt lost, uncertain, and even intimidated by my surroundings. I remember sitting in the classroom surrounded by students who seemed so confident with computers, while I struggled even to identify the basic parts of a system. It felt like being a fish out of water.In the first few weeks, my self-confidence began to waver. Every class felt like a test of survival. When the faculty used terms like “motherboard,” “BIOS,” “IP address,” or “Python compiler,” I found myself quietly confused while others nodded with understanding. I hesitated to ask questions, afraid of appearing naïve or slow. There were moments when I genuinely questioned whether I had made the right choice. Cybersecurity felt so far from my comfort zone that I even thought about giving up. But life has a way of sending the right people at the right time — and in my case, that person was Nivedita, my classmate and the one who sat beside me in every class.From the very beginning, Nivedita noticed my hesitation. Instead of ignoring it, she went out of her way to help me understand the basics. I still remember one particular day when we were asked to identify computer components during a practical session. I stared blankly at the monitor and CPU, unsure where to begin. Without a hint of judgment, she leaned over and said, “It’s okay, I’ll show you.” That simple sentence instantly made me feel included. She began explaining everything — from hardware parts like RAM and hard drives to software settings, file types, and shortcuts. She broke down each topic in a way that made it easy to grasp, always ensuring I was following along before moving to the next point.Her patience was extraordinary. What touched me the most was that she never made me feel inferior for not knowing something. Instead, she treated me as an equal, encouraging me with every small progress I made. Slowly, with her guidance, I started gaining confidence. Tasks that once felt overwhelming — like setting up system configurations, using commands, or understanding basic programming logic — began to make sense.One day, when I expressed my interest in learning programming, particularly Python, Nivedita did something I’ll never forget. She offered me access to her Python learning account so I could explore coding lessons at my own pace. This act of generosity moved me deeply. In a world where people often hesitate to share resources, she trusted me enough to share her personal account. That single gesture showed her genuine willingness to help me grow.Through her support, I began spending extra hours practicing basic coding exercises. I learned to print outputs, write small programs, and understand the logic behind each step. Slowly, I began developing a curiosity for the subject that I never thought I would have. What was once intimidating started to become interesting. The fear that had once held me back transformed into motivation. I began to look forward to classes instead of dreading them.Looking back now, I realize that this entire journey taught me much more than just technical knowledge — it taught me humility, gratitude, and the importance of helping others without expecting anything in return. Nivedita became more than a classmate; she became a mentor and a friend who reminded me that learning is not about where you start, but how willing you are to keep going.There were moments when I still struggled — times when I couldn’t understand a concept no matter how hard I tried. But instead of feeling hopeless, I would now remind myself that it’s okay to be a beginner. Every expert was once a learner. That mindset shift happened because of Nivedita’s constant encouragement. She never allowed me to give up on myself.This experience also taught me how kindness can make a powerful impact in academic life. One person’s patience and support can completely change another person’s confidence and outlook. In my case, Nivedita’s kindness helped me move from confusion to clarity, from fear to curiosity. Whenever I successfully completed a task that once scared me — such as writing my first Python program or configuring a basic computer setting — I silently thanked her in my heart.Gratitude, I realized, isn’t just about saying “thank you.” It’s about truly acknowledging the difference someone makes in your journey. I often think about how different my experience would have been if Nivedita hadn’t been there beside me. I might have continued to feel lost, isolated, and insecure. Instead, I found a sense of belonging and confidence.Another valuable lesson I learned through this experience is that gratitude creates a ripple effect. The kindness I received made me want to help others who might feel the same way someday. Now, whenever a classmate struggles with something — even a small concept — I make sure to explain it patiently, just like Nivedita did for me. In doing so, I feel that I am passing forward the same warmth and guidance that once lifted me.Today, I can say with pride that although I come from a biological background, I have adapted successfully to a technology-based field. I have learned to navigate computer systems, write programs, and approach cybersecurity topics with curiosity instead of fear. This growth didn’t happen overnight — it happened because someone believed in me and invested their time and trust. For that, I am sincerely grateful.Reflecting on this experience, I now understand that true learning extends beyond textbooks. It is also about empathy, collaboration, and human connection. No amount of theoretical knowledge can replace the impact of genuine kindness. I will always remember how Nivedita’s guidance changed my perception of myself. She showed me that being new to something doesn’t make you weak — it simply means you are brave enough to start.In conclusion, what began as a period of confusion and insecurity turned into one of the most enriching experiences of my academic life. I learned that gratitude is not limited to grand achievements; it often lies in the small, consistent acts of help that others offer us. My friend Nivedita’s patience, generosity, and belief in me reignited my confidence and curiosity. I am deeply thankful for her presence in my life and for the lesson she unknowingly taught me — that when we lift others, we all rise together.This scenario reminded me that gratitude is not just an emotion but a way of living. It helps us see the light even when we are surrounded by uncertainty. And now, whenever I switch on my computer, write a piece of code, or solve a technical problem successfully, I remember where I started — and the friend who helped me believe I could learn anything. 

In conclusion, I am truly grateful for the people, experiences, and lessons that have shaped me. Each moment of kindness and support reminds me how fortunate I am to be surrounded by such positivity.
